## Executive Summary

ISO/SAE 21434:2021 is the engineering standard that the automotive industry adopted to operationalise cybersecurity across the road-vehicle lifecycle. Where UN R155 (companion document) is the regulatory frame that vehicle manufacturers must satisfy for type approval, ISO/SAE 21434 is the engineering recipe — it specifies the cybersecurity work products that must exist, the lifecycle phases in which they are produced, and the management-system context in which they are governed.

SCOUT addresses the technically demanding portions of the standard's evidence requirements — specifically vulnerability analysis, software bill of materials, attack-surface inventory, hardening attestation, and the cybersecurity event monitoring data feed — through hash-anchored evidence packaging, machine-readable output, and deterministic pipeline reproducibility. The cybersecurity management system itself, the threat analysis and risk assessment (TARA) narrative, and the post-development continuous cybersecurity activities remain the manufacturer's responsibility; SCOUT supplies the evidence inputs that those activities consume.

This document maps SCOUT's analytical capabilities to the ISO/SAE 21434 work products that are most amenable to tool-driven evidence generation, and explicitly identifies the work products that remain manual or organisational.

## Standard Structure

ISO/SAE 21434:2021 is organised into 15 normative clauses. The clauses most relevant to firmware-level evidence are:

| Clause | Title |
|--------|-------|
| 5 | Organisational cybersecurity management |
| 6 | Project-dependent cybersecurity management |
| 7 | Distributed cybersecurity activities |
| 8 | Continual cybersecurity activities |
| 9 | Concept |
| 10 | Product development |
| 11 | Cybersecurity validation |
| 12 | Production |
| 13 | Operations and maintenance |
| 14 | End of cybersecurity support and decommissioning |
| 15 | Threat analysis and risk assessment (TARA) methods |

The standard works by listing per-clause **work products** (designated `WP-xx-yy`) that must exist for an item or component. SCOUT's outputs map onto a defined subset of those work products.

## SBOM Considerations

ISO/SAE 21434 does not name a specific SBOM format, but Clause 8 (continual cybersecurity activities) and Clause 13 (operations and maintenance) require the manufacturer to know what software components are in the deployed product so vulnerability monitoring can be performed against them. SCOUT's CycloneDX 1.6 SBOM (`stages/sbom/sbom.json`) provides the inventory, and the same SBOM is referenced by the EU CRA mapping (B-1) and the FDA Section 524B mapping (B-2). Using the same SBOM artefact across all three regulatory regimes simplifies cross-jurisdiction evidence packaging.

## Coverage Gaps and Limitations

The following items are explicitly **outside SCOUT's scope** and remain manufacturer-side responsibilities:

1. **Cybersecurity management system (CSMS).** Organisational policies, role assignments, interface agreements with suppliers, and competence management.
2. **Item definition narrative.** SCOUT can describe the binaries; it cannot describe the item's intended use, operational environment, or vehicle-level role.
3. **TARA narrative and methodology.** SCOUT supplies inputs (attack surface, graphs, vulnerability inventory) but does not perform TARA itself.
4. **Cybersecurity goals, claims, and specifications.** These are manufacturer-authored design-level artefacts.
5. **Production process controls.** Outside the firmware analysis scope.
6. **Incident response and end-of-support plans.** Organisational deliverables.
7. **Independent assessment.** ISO/SAE 21434 conformity may be subject to independent assessment under Clause 6.4.9; that assessment is performed against the manufacturer's complete CSMS, not against tool outputs in isolation.
