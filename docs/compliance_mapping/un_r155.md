## Executive Summary

UN Regulation No. 155 applies to vehicles of categories M and N (passenger cars, light commercial vehicles, heavy commercial vehicles), and where fitted with at least one electronic control unit, also categories O₃ and O₄ (trailers). Two separate approvals are required:

1. **Cyber Security Management System (CSMS) approval** — issued at the manufacturer level, valid for three years, covering the manufacturer's organisational and process arrangements for managing cybersecurity throughout the vehicle lifecycle.
2. **Vehicle type approval (cybersecurity)** — issued per vehicle type, requiring evidence that the type-specific cybersecurity risks have been identified, assessed, and treated, and that the manufacturer's CSMS approval covers the type.

The technical evidence underpinning the vehicle type approval — risk assessment results, mitigation effectiveness verification, and post-production monitoring inputs — is exactly the territory where SCOUT-style firmware analysis output is most relevant. SCOUT does not produce the CSMS narrative or perform the type-approval assessment; it produces the per-firmware evidence that those activities consume.

## Regulatory Structure

UN R155's substantive obligations are concentrated in:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| 7.2 | CSMS requirements (organisational processes, role assignments, supplier interface, monitoring) |
| 7.3 | Vehicle type cybersecurity requirements (risk assessment, mitigations, verification, monitoring) |
| 7.4 | Reporting requirements (annual reporting on monitoring activities and detected attacks) |
| Annex 5 | Threats catalogue (Part A: vehicle-level threats, Part B: outside-vehicle threats), example mitigations (Part B), security measures (Part C) |
| Annex 6 | Reporting templates |

The Annex 5 threats catalogue is the most concrete element: it enumerates 32 vehicle-related threat categories (e.g. communication channel manipulation, ECU firmware tampering, denial-of-service, software supply chain attacks). The vehicle type approval requires the manufacturer to demonstrate, for each Annex 5 threat applicable to the type, that the risk is identified and treated.

## Section 7.2 (CSMS) Mapping

The CSMS approval is a process-level approval issued against the manufacturer's organisational arrangements. SCOUT outputs are not directly evaluated in the CSMS approval, but they are referenced as evidence that the manufacturer's processes work in practice.

## Annex 5 Threats Catalogue

UN R155 Annex 5 lists 32 vehicle-related threat categories. SCOUT findings can be tagged against the most relevant categories during analyst review; the table below indicates which threat categories SCOUT outputs are most likely to surface evidence for. **This is not an automated tagging — it is a guide for analysts performing the type-approval evidence review.**

| Annex 5 Threat (paraphrased) | SCOUT relevance |
|------------------------------|-----------------|
| 4.3.1 — Spoofing of messages or data received by the vehicle | `enhanced_source` (input API inventory), `taint_propagation` (input → sink reachability) |
| 4.3.2 — Communication channels used to conduct unauthorised manipulation | `attack_surface`, `endpoints`, `surfaces`, `graph` |
| 4.3.3 — Replay attack against communication gateway | `enhanced_source`, `cve_scan` (replay-prone protocols, cryptographic library CVEs) |
| 4.3.4 — Manipulation by an authorised user | `fs_permissions`, `init_analysis`, `cert_analysis` |
| 4.3.5 — Information leak from vehicle to outside party | `enhanced_source`, `endpoints`, `cert_analysis` |
| 4.3.6 — Manipulation or tampering with vehicle-held data | `findings`, `inventory` (hardening — RELRO/canary mitigations) |
| 4.3.7 — Loss of integrity for safety-critical functions | `findings`, `taint_propagation`, `fp_verification` |
| 4.3.8 — Manipulation of code or data | `findings`, `inventory` (NX/PIE hardening) |
| 4.3.9 — Insertion of malware or compromised software | `sbom`, `cve_scan`, `findings` |
| 4.3.10 — Software vulnerabilities in development | `findings`, `cve_scan`, `taint_propagation`, `enhanced_source` |
| 4.3.11 — Network-design vulnerabilities | `attack_surface`, `graph`, `endpoints` |
| 4.3.12 — Unintentional transfer of data | `enhanced_source`, `endpoints` |
| 4.3.13 — Physical manipulation of systems | — (out of scope; physical security is not a firmware-analysis question) |
| 4.3.14 — Manipulation of vehicle telematics | `attack_surface`, `endpoints`, `cert_analysis` |
| 4.3.15 — Use of unauthorised internal interface | `init_analysis`, `fs_permissions`, `attack_surface` |

The remaining Annex 5 categories (4.3.16 through 4.3.32) are addressed primarily through process-level evidence (CSMS) rather than per-firmware analysis output and are outside SCOUT's direct scope.

## Coverage Gaps and Limitations

The following items are explicitly **outside SCOUT's scope** and remain manufacturer-side responsibilities:

1. **CSMS organisational deliverables.** Governance, role assignments, supplier interface contracts, training records.
2. **Vehicle-level architecture context.** SCOUT analyses individual firmware artefacts; vehicle-architecture composition (multi-ECU integration, in-vehicle network topology) is a manufacturer-side activity.
3. **Type-approval test report.** Manufacturer authors the type-approval test report; SCOUT outputs are inputs.
4. **Forensic and incident-response capabilities.** SCOUT analyses pre-deployment firmware, not post-incident artefacts.
5. **Vehicle-runtime logging design.** Out of scope.
6. **Annex 6 reporting templates.** SCOUT does not pre-fill Annex 6 templates; the analyst-digest output supplies the underlying findings the templates require.
7. **Independent assessment.** UN R155 type approvals are issued by Approval Authorities (national vehicle type approval authorities under the 1958 Agreement); the assessment is performed against the manufacturer's complete documentation, not against tool outputs in isolation.
